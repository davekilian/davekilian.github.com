I love computer systems. I have experience in computer graphics, game engine design, operating system development and computer networking. I've written a SQL query or two and have built a few server-centric web apps.

My current areas of interest include in-browser development, game development, and computer graphics (especially teaching it). The latter is fueled by my work as the head teaching assistant for both of Brown's computer graphics classes (CS123 and CS224).

My Story

I was first introduced to Google as a middle-schooler looking for video game cheat codes. I got a whole lot more than I bargained for.

My first experiences traversing the 'net fostered a deep interest in computers and computing. I learned what was inside my computer, and assembled one from those parts. I learned how to use search operators to efficiently find whatever I needed. And I became obsessed with freeware. For a period of several months, I used up all the CPU time and disk space on the family computer in my quest to find the perfect settings for a DVD-ripping application I had found online.

As an avid free software user and Windows power user, I was naturally inclined to start producing freeware myself. After one or two false starts, I found myself proficient in writing C# programs. I learned all about the .NET technologies of the day, including Winforms, XNA, and MSSQL. With a strong foundation in C#, I had an easy time picking up Java and doing some work in Swing. I would later pass the AP CS exam with flying colors.

At the same time, I got hooked on Security Now!, a podcast about computer security. The very first episode I listened to was an explanation of the call stack and buffer overflows. Thanks to Security Now!, I accidentally got a feel for machine architecture before I ever wrote a line of code. I owe a lot of my early knowledge of computer underpinnings to the study of system security. And to Steve Gibson.

Several months into my programming career, a friend and I came up with a ludicrous idea for a game called Newman Vs Newman. It was best described as 2D dodgeball with guns, where you play as Newman (from Seinfeld) on his quest to stop Paul Newman and his clone army from obtaining world domination. I ended up with a good reason to port the prototype from C#/XNA to C++/DirectX, and successfully did so despite lacking basic knowledge (at the time, I didn't understand why some C++ types had asterisks next to them ... a lot of sweat and frustration went into that port)

I had the good fortune of being selected to attend the Virginia Summer Governor's School for Math, Science, and Technology. I chose to take a class on 3D graphics and game programming there, and met the friend with whom I would later found Fracture. Our two-week C++ prototype of nullZERO could be classified a failure in more ways than one; yet we came out knowing what we wanted to build, and we were determined to learn how to do it right.

Back in high school during my senior year, my programming story took an unexpected turn toward the world of web servers, FOSS, and scripting languages. One day after a friend walked into class and drew a funny comic or two on the white board, I decided to make them an online home. I built Peebles Lab (named after our high school's physics classroom) from scratch using Apache, PHP, and MySQL. We were able to run 100 comics and gain a smidgeon of online fame before we got too busy to continue writing and drawing comics.

In 2009 I was accepted into Brown University's class of 2013, and what a ride it's been! At Brown I met many new people and learned so many new things. I consider my education at Brown indispensible. I took classes in a wide range of subjects, from engineering topics like graphics, OSes and networking to more theoretical topics like algorithms, NLP and a brief stint programming functionally.

Possibly even more awesome than the things I've learned in class have been the things I've learned out of class. Although Brown CS isn't a huge department by any means, it's chock-full of smart people with lots to share. Over time, my experience molded me into a vim-wielding hacker with the sort of breadth that I would have never been able to teach myself.

During my undergrad, I TA'ed a total of five classes: software engineering, computer security, the undergrad class on computer graphics (once as a normal undergrad TA and once as the head TA, who leads the team), and the graduate course on computer graphics. Through my experience TAing, I gained valuable experience developing software and working in teams. I also got a chance to practice my passion of sharing my knowledge with others.

After two internships at Microsoft, I am signed on to work with Microsoft full-time. I've yet to see where the future takes me, but things look exciting!
